Overview

ART (Academic Reporting Tool) is a web application that provides academic statistics about courses, majors and instructors. ART has been informing academic decision making at U-M since 2006. The ART2.0 suite is a reboot of the traditional service with a mission to expand access, especially to students, and to broaden the scope of the data offered while respecting student privacy.

Where everything begins

Introduction

On May 7th 2018,  I worked as an UX design fellow at Academic Innovation(AI) in Ann Arbor, Michigan.  
‍
I was introduced to this project called Academic Reporting Tool (ART) - a data visualization tool that aims to help students make better and informed academic decisions.
‍
Originally, it was an idea of a physics professor that seeks for a change in the traditional academic information obtainment among students and faculties. which was how ART has been created in the first place.

Problem

As time goes by since it was first launched in 2006, Things start to get disorganized and outdated given modern standard. I have participated in the project that aims to redesign the entire platform to bring back its charm.

Project Background

ART has both students and faculties in mind

ART was designed for both students and faculties in mind, there are over 40,000 students and 4,500 facilities in University of Michigan. Thousands of courses are offered across all fields and domains. For students, staying informative about popular majors and courses that align with their learning objectives is highly desired. For faculties, seeing the bigger landscape as well as identifying patterns and trend are crucial for making decisions that impact the vast student population.

Design Objectives

Our team decided to focus on four major domain task to rejuvenate ART.

After numerous internal meeting and heuristic evaluation, we have decided to focus on four areas to improve the holistic experience of ART  

UI/Interaction design refresh

Over years, many of the visual language start to feel outdated, we decided to have a complete visual overhaul and interaction redesign.

Develop new features to assist both students and faculties

Conduct interviews among faculties and students to understand their needs and expectation. Develop new features that accommodate their daily academic lives.

Prioritize and improve information architecture

Conduct user testing to understand how current user use ART, change the information hierarchy and get rid of redundant and less useful information

Update existing visualization and introduce new ones

Redesign the current visualization, meanwhile introduce more visualizations that closely align with user’s needs

User research

Putting H in the HCI.

We conducted 8 interviews, 5 of the participants are undergrad students ranging from 1st year to 3rd year. 3 Interviews among faculties, program administrative lead and course registration office lead. We have created 4 personas to better reflect the nuances of their needs.

Alex Green - 1st Year

Being a newly admitted undergrad student, Alex is still adapting to college life and often has no clue about what courses to take. His decision about whether or not to take a course mostly relies on his peer's feedback and the grade distribution.

David Brandon - 2nd Year

David has multiple sources to get academic information, with ART being one of them, David would  check ART during class registration period to look for courses that align with his interest. Meanwhile, he is still debating about what major he should pursue at the end of the second year.

Jessica Murray - 3rd Year

Jessica is a junior student from school of information majoring in data science  She is more interested in taking courses that would prepare her for future career,  so that she would usually ask people from her program about what courses that they are taking.

Mark Hanson - Physic Professor

Mark is a renown professor and is respected by many students. When teaching a course, he is often curious about students' background so that he could adjust the content of his course to accommodate more students. Besides, he would like to know student’s performance in terms of grade from year to year to quickly identify patterns.;

User Research

Identify Where Problem Occurs.

In order to fully understand where users, specifically students, struggle the most, first we analyzed user's traffic and composition, which indicates that the peak usually occur during mid November, late April and late September, which are usually around the time when class registration is taking place and students are debating what courses to take. To better identify pain points and design spaces during this time,  we mapped out the regular class registration flow.

1. Easily get biased by the source of information

Students are referring many sources to obtain information, some of the sources are questionable as the information might be extremely biased, therefore affecting student's decision negatively.

2.Use various kinds of tool to build tentative schedule

After confirming what courses to take, students use a variety of tools, such as notebook, google calendar, excel and etc to build tentative schedule. This process is usually not very efficient, hard to keep track of and might not be accurate if a course has a last minute schedule change.

3.Constantly check the registration system for open seats.

Sometimes, a desired courses could be fully registered and student would no longer be able to register, which results in students constantly logging to the registration system to check available open seats.

Key Findings

After getting the information, what's next?

Students
1, The major motivation of using ART is to look for potential courses to take, the traffic on ART usually goes off the roof during class registration season.
2. Want to know what majors they might be potential qualified for (mostly first and second year undergrads)
3. Have trouble building a tentative schedule for the upcoming semester as the current tool that school provides is not very easy to use.(students could do it via Wolverine Access, but it is really slow and complicated so people rarely use it)

Faculty
1. Want to know where students are coming from and going while or after taking their course.
2. Want to See a bigger landscape, like student’s overall academic performance in their courses, and demographic information.
3. Generally really care about their evaluation in comparison with other faculties.

Mapping hierarchy of information for existing system.

We first mapped out the existing IA of ART,  it is fairly straightforward but at the same time very poorly structured, for instance, there s only one entry point to all the pages, which is the search bar. and information on instructor page and falsity page is relatively deficient.

Information Architecture.

Regroup and prioritize the existing visualization

With the help of the data scientist on the team and also the feedback from the usability test, we have decided to eliminate some of the visualization of ART to make it more efficient and effective in delivering the information that users desire.

Set MVP goals for new features.

Information Architecture

New IA that has improved efficiency, hierarchy and aligns better with user’s mental model  

Taking into account of the new features, we have finalized the updated information architecture that enhances efficiency of finding desired information. the purple ones represent new feature, black ones represent redesign of the existing features.

Design Exploration

Setting principles for data visualization to reach a balance between expressiveness and effectiveness.

Given our targets user being both students and faculties, it is a challenge to develop visualization that is both easy to interpret at first glance and also capable of offering detailed information if user decide to delve deeper.

Design Exploration

We keep experimenting different ways of visualization.

For each visualization, we make sure it is both efficient in getting the data and also expressive in a way that everybody could understand, Take the the bar chart for example, we have explored many alternatives to encode data.

Usability Test

We make sure validation with users is at at step of the way.

In order to make sure everything works as expected from user’s perspective, a lot testing techniques have been adopted, we have conducted in-house AB testing as well as a speed testing on the street. The result is very satisfactory as we incorporated a variety of feedback from different target demographic.
